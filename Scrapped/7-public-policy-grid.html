Chapter 7: Public Policy and Government in Action

Policy Process

üìù Public Policy

A course of action or inaction chosen by public authorities to address a problem. Public policies can take various forms, including laws, regulations, executive orders, and court decisions.

‚öôÔ∏è Policy Cycle

A conceptual model of how policies are developed, implemented, and evaluated. Typically includes stages such as agenda setting, policy formulation, adoption, implementation, evaluation, and termination or renewal.

üìã Agenda Setting

The process by which problems come to the attention of government officials and are considered for possible action. Issues can reach the agenda through media attention, interest group activity, elected officials, or crisis events.

üìä Policy Formulation

The development of proposed solutions to policy problems. This stage involves research, analysis, and debate about potential options for addressing the identified issue.

‚úÖ Policy Adoption

The stage where a specific policy option is chosen and formalized through legislation, executive action, or other means. This often involves negotiation and compromise among different stakeholders.

üèóÔ∏è Policy Implementation

The process of putting policies into practice. This stage involves the actions of bureaucratic agencies, which often have discretion in how they carry out policies established by elected officials.

üîç Policy Evaluation

Assessment of whether a policy has achieved its intended goals. May lead to policy continuation, modification, or termination. Can be conducted by government agencies, independent researchers, or oversight bodies.

üß† Think Tanks

Research organizations that conduct policy analysis and advocate for particular approaches to public problems. Examples include the Brookings Institution, Heritage Foundation, and Center for American Progress.

üìö Policy Analysis

Systematic evaluation of the technical and political dimensions of public policy. Involves defining problems, identifying alternatives, predicting consequences, and recommending optimal solutions.

üß© Incrementalism

A policy-making approach characterized by small, gradual changes rather than comprehensive reforms. Often described as "muddling through," it reflects the practical and political constraints on policy-making.

Economic Policy

üíµ Fiscal Policy

The use of government spending and taxation to influence the economy. Used to promote economic growth, combat inflation, or address other economic objectives. Primary responsibility of Congress and the President.

üè¶ Monetary Policy

The regulation of the money supply and interest rates to achieve economic goals. Controlled by the Federal Reserve, which can expand or contract the money supply through various tools like setting interest rates.

üìà Federal Budget

The annual financial plan for the federal government, detailing expected revenues and planned expenditures. The President proposes a budget, but Congress has the ultimate authority to approve spending.

üîÑ Budget Process

The annual cycle for developing and approving the federal budget. Begins with the President's budget proposal, followed by Congressional budget resolution, appropriations bills, and ongoing oversight.

üìâ Budget Deficit

Occurs when government spending exceeds revenue in a fiscal year. Must be financed by borrowing, which adds to the national debt. Deficits may be used to stimulate economic growth during recessions.

üí∞ National Debt

The total amount the federal government owes to creditors, accumulated through annual deficits. Consists of bonds and securities held by individuals, corporations, foreign governments, and government trust funds.

üí∏ Progressive Taxation

A tax system where the tax rate increases as income increases. The federal income tax is progressive, with higher earners paying a larger percentage of their income in taxes.

üõí Regressive Taxation

A tax system where the tax rate decreases as income increases. Sales taxes are often regressive because lower-income households spend a larger portion of their income on taxable goods.

üíº Corporate Tax

Tax levied on the profits of corporations. The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 reduced the corporate tax rate from 35% to 21%, representing a significant shift in U.S. tax policy.

üî® Monetary Tools

Mechanisms used by the Federal Reserve to control the money supply, including open market operations, the discount rate, and reserve requirements. Used to manage inflation, employment, and economic growth.

Social Policy

üè• Healthcare Policy

Government decisions regarding the provision, regulation, and financing of healthcare services. Major programs include Medicare, Medicaid, and the Affordable Care Act (Obamacare).

üëµ Social Security

Federal program providing benefits to retired workers, disabled persons, and survivors of deceased workers. Funded through payroll taxes and operates as a pay-as-you-go system rather than a fully funded pension.

üè´ Education Policy

Government decisions regarding educational systems, standards, funding, and access. In the U.S., education policy is primarily set at state and local levels, with some federal oversight and funding.

üßì Medicare

Federal health insurance program primarily for those aged 65 and older. Consists of Part A (hospital insurance), Part B (medical insurance), Part C (Medicare Advantage), and Part D (prescription drug coverage).

ü§≤ Medicaid

Joint federal-state program providing health coverage for low-income individuals. States administer the program under federal guidelines but have significant flexibility in eligibility and benefits.

üß™ Affordable Care Act

Comprehensive healthcare reform law passed in 2010, aimed at expanding coverage, improving quality, and reducing costs. Key provisions include insurance market reforms, individual mandate (repealed in 2019), and Medicaid expansion.

üçé No Child Left Behind

Education law enacted in 2002 that increased federal influence over K-12 education through testing requirements, accountability provisions, and school improvement measures. Later replaced by the Every Student Succeeds Act.

üìö Every Student Succeeds Act

2015 law that replaced No Child Left Behind, maintaining standardized testing requirements but returning more control over education policy to states and local districts.

üè† Housing Policy

Government efforts to ensure access to safe, affordable housing. Includes programs like Section 8 housing vouchers, public housing, and the Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD).

üíä Drug Policy

Government approaches to controlling drug production, distribution, and use. In recent years, some states have moved toward decriminalization or legalization of marijuana, creating tension with federal policy.

Foreign Policy

üåé Foreign Policy

The strategies and approaches governments use to guide their actions in the international arena. U.S. foreign policy aims to protect national security, promote economic interests, and advance democratic values.

üïäÔ∏è Diplomacy

The practice of conducting negotiations and maintaining relations between nations. Conducted primarily by the State Department and diplomatic corps, led by the Secretary of State under direction from the President.

üõ°Ô∏è National Security

The protection of a nation against threats to its existence, sovereignty, and interests. U.S. national security policy involves multiple agencies, including the Department of Defense, intelligence community, and State Department.

üåê Multilateralism

An approach to foreign policy that emphasizes cooperation among multiple countries. Involves working through international organizations like the United Nations, NATO, and the World Trade Organization.

üèõÔ∏è Unilateralism

An approach to foreign policy where a country acts independently without coordination with other nations. Critics argue this undermines international cooperation; supporters emphasize flexibility and sovereignty.

ü§ù Treaties

Formal agreements between nations. Under the U.S. Constitution, treaties require approval by two-thirds of the Senate. Executive agreements, which don't require Senate approval, are increasingly used as alternatives.

üí∞ Foreign Aid

Economic and military assistance provided to other countries. Can take many forms, including direct grants, loans, technical assistance, and military equipment. Often used to advance foreign policy objectives.

üîÑ Trade Policy

Government policies regarding imports and exports. May include tariffs, quotas, subsidies, and trade agreements. In the U.S., trade policy involves both the President and Congress.

üõ¢Ô∏è Sanctions

Economic penalties imposed on countries, entities, or individuals to influence their behavior. Can include asset freezes, travel bans, trade restrictions, and limits on financial transactions.

üöÅ Military Intervention

The use of armed forces in another country. May range from limited operations to full-scale warfare. Under the Constitution, Congress has the power to declare war, but presidents have often deployed forces without formal declarations.

Environmental Policy

üå± Environmental Protection Agency

Federal agency created in 1970 to protect human health and the environment through regulation and enforcement. Implements major environmental laws and develops regulations on issues like air and water pollution.

üåßÔ∏è Clean Air Act

Federal law that regulates air emissions to protect public health and the environment. Authorizes the EPA to establish National Ambient Air Quality Standards and regulate hazardous air pollutants.

üíß Clean Water Act

Federal law that regulates water pollution. Establishes a system of water quality standards, discharge limitations, and permitting requirements for point source pollutants.

‚ò¢Ô∏è Superfund

Program established by the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act to clean up sites contaminated with hazardous substances. Funded by taxes on chemical and petroleum industries.

üå°Ô∏è Climate Policy

Government actions to address climate change through mitigation (reducing greenhouse gas emissions) and adaptation (preparing for climate impacts). Has been politically contentious in the U.S.

üåç Paris Agreement

International treaty on climate change adopted in 2015. Sets goals for limiting global warming and requires countries to submit national plans for reducing emissions. The U.S. withdrew under President Trump but rejoined under President Biden.

ü¶Ö Endangered Species Act

Federal law designed to protect imperiled species from extinction. Prohibits the "taking" of listed species and requires federal agencies to ensure their actions do not jeopardize listed species or their habitats.

üå≤ National Parks

Protected areas managed by the National Park Service for conservation and public enjoyment. The system includes over 400 areas covering more than 85 million acres.

‚ö° Energy Policy

Government decisions regarding energy production, distribution, and consumption. Involves considerations of security, environmental impact, and economic development. Has shifted over time with changing technologies and priorities.

‚ôªÔ∏è Regulatory Tools

Instruments used to implement environmental policy, including command-and-control regulations, market-based mechanisms (like cap-and-trade), subsidies, and information disclosure requirements.
