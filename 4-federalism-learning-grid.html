Chapter 4: Federalism

Federalism Basics

üèõÔ∏è Federalism

A system of government in which powers are divided between a national government and regional government. It allows for shared power, dual sovereignty and creates a compound republic.

‚úÖ Advantages of Federalism

It prevents abuse of power, reflects preferences of citizens, is more responsive to citizens, and allows for innovation - states can act as laboratories of democracy.

‚ùå Disadvantages of Federalism

Inequities are the price of federalism. There are often coordination problems and confusion. Also a race to the bottom - in which states purposely do little to improve standing so they can receive aid.

üî¨ Laboratories of Democracy

Term coined by Supreme Court Justice Louis Brandeis meaning voters in states could implement policies to see if they work at the state level, before they are implemented at the national levels. Examples include California limiting greenhouse gas emissions, states funding stem cell research, and expanding health care.

üèôÔ∏è Local Governments

Local governments are not included in the definition of the state. They are creatures of the state.

‚öñÔ∏è Supremacy Clause

National laws and treaties are always superior to state laws whenever the two clash.

Types of Federalism

üç∞ Dual Federalism (Layer Cake)

Prominent from 1787-1932, it's the idea that states and federal government have separate domains (different layers of a cake). It's a clear division of governing authority between the national and state governments.

üç° Cooperative Federalism (Marble Cake)

Prominent from 1933-1964, it's the idea that both levels of government should work together. The federal government should identify problems and provide funding to states to solve problems. Functions overlap across governments.

üß† Moral Federalism

According to Rauch, moral federalism means leaving states free to go their separate ways when a national moral consensus is lacking.

Federal-State Relations

ü•ï Carrots and Sticks

The government gets states to act as they wish through incentives. The carrots are grants in aid (categorical or block), but in order to receive grants they must pass mandates.

‚ö° Preemptions

The invalidation of state law that conflicts with federal law. (Example: sports gambling)

üöß Cross-over Sanctions

A technique of Congress to establish federal regulations. These sanctions permit the use of federal money in one program to influence state and local policy in another (example: drinking age or highway trust fund).

üîÑ Partial Preemptions

Federal government's assumption of some regulatory powers in a particular field, with the stipulation that a state law on the same subject as a federal law is valid if it does not conflict with the federal law in the same area (example: ACA).

üõ†Ô∏è State Responses to Federal Action

States can reject federal dollars, take the federal government to court, stall or refuse to act, or lobby for change in the federal law.

üë®‚Äç‚öñÔ∏è Arbiter of Federal-State Disputes

The Supreme Court. They interpret Federal-State Activities.

Constitutional Provisions

üìú 10th Amendment

Reserves to states all powers not expressly granted to the government branches.

üõí Commerce Clause

Allows the federal government to regulate trade between states. Court findings have flipped-flopped significantly, but gravitated towards a weaker federal role.

ü§ù Full Faith and Credit

The constitutional requirement (in article 1, section 4) that each state recognize and uphold laws passed by any other state. Important exceptions include gay marriage and gun laws.

üîë Privileges and Immunities

Prevents a state from treating citizens of other states in a discriminatory manner. An example is McDonald v Chicago - said right to keep and bear arms is incorporated by the 14th amendment is applied to the states.

üìã State Powers in the Constitution

States can amend the constitution, propose a constitutional convention, elect senators (at the beginning), elect electors and form interstate compacts.

üìù Enumerated Federal Powers

Government can only use the powers specifically listed in the Constitution. Important to federalism because they define federal authority.

üîÑ Concurrent Powers

Governmental authority shared by national and state governments, such as the power to tax residents. These include power to tax, borrow and spend.

Landmark Cases

‚öñÔ∏è McCulloch v. Maryland (1819)

It ruled Congress had implied powers granted to it by elastic clause - it granted Congress the authority to make all laws which shall be necessary and proper for carrying into execution the work of the federal government without state's attacking.

üî´ McDonald v. Chicago

Said right to keep and bear arms is incorporated by the 14th amendment and applied to the states.

üíß Chattahoochee River Dispute

An example of federalism that raises the issue of interstate boundaries - who has proper right to use what amount of the water.

Contemporary Issues

üíí Gay Marriage

Federal Defense of Marriage Act was an issue where federal government and state governments battled over powers. An exception to full faith and credit.

üè• Health Care

Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act is an example of federal and state governments battling over powers. A case of partial preemption.

üß≥ Immigration

AZ Immigration Laws are an example of federal and state governments battling over powers granted to the federal government over the state government.

üë• Pluralism

A system of government in which multiple competing and responsive groups vie for power. How James Madison controlled factions according to the Federalist Papers.

üèõÔ∏è Republic

System of government that rests ultimate governing power in its people, who rule via representatives. According to Madison, a republic can better protect liberties than a democracy because the majority cannot easily overwhelm the minority.
