Enigma Cipher Questions

Fall 2022 Quiz #2

(6 pts) Briefly describe the roles that Hans-Thilo Schmidt and Marian Rejewski played in relation to the Enigma machine/code used by the Germans before and during World War II. (A single sentence for each will suffice.)

Answer:

Hans-Thilo Schmidt, a German spy, provided the French with information on the Enigma machine's daily settings, which proved to be invaluable for its eventual decryption. Marian Rejewski was a Polish mathematician who used this information, along with his mathematical genius, to reverse-engineer the Enigma machine's wiring and create a method to find its daily settings without having a physical machine.
Fall 2021 Quiz #2

(6 pts) Imagine an Engima for the Spanish alphabet back in the 1930s, when Spanish had 30 letters. Given 5 rotors to choose from (the version of the Engima used during WWII), how many settings (different configurations for encryption, so ultimately potentially different substitution ciphers) could the machine be in (ignoring the plugboard)? (In your answer, just consider which rotors can be placed in which of the spots as well as the possible rotations of the rotors.) Express your answer in scientific notation.

Answer: $$1.62 \times 10^6$$

The number of settings is the number of ways to arrange the rotors multiplied by the number of rotations for each rotor. The number of ways to arrange 3 rotors from a set of 5 is a permutation: $$P(5, 3) = 5! / (5-3)! = 5 \times 4 \times 3 = 60$$ The number of rotations for each of the 3 rotors is 30, since the Spanish alphabet has 30 letters. The number of rotations is $$30^3$$ Total settings = $$P(5, 3) \times 30^3 = 60 \times 27000 = 1,620,000$$ The answer in scientific notation is $$1.62 \times 10^6$$
Fall 2020 Quiz #1

(6 pts) Consider an Enigma for an alternative language with 10 symbols in its alphabet and four slots for rotors instead of three. Also, imagine that to fill the slots for the rotors, there were a total of 6 potential rotors to be placed. Assume that similar look up charts could be constructed (to the ones that Rejewski had constructed), one for each possible rotor placement and rotation of each of the rotors. How many different charts would need to be calculated?

Answer: $$3,600,000$$

The number of different charts is the number of ways to arrange the rotors multiplied by the number of rotations for each rotor. Number of rotor arrangements = $$P(6, 4) = 6! / (6-4)! = 6 \times 5 \times 4 \times 3 = 360$$ Number of rotor rotations = $$10^4 = 10,000$$ Total number of charts = $$360 \times 10,000 = 3,600,000$$
